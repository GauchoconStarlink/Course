📈 The Breakout & Retest

Class 34: Entering a Fresh Breakout at Low Risk
Phase 7 · Strategies & Edges · Crypto · Breakout

📚 What It Is & Why It Works

The breakout & retest is how you join a fresh breakout without chasing it. Price breaks a level everyone is watching, then comes back to test that level from above — and if the old ceiling now acts as a floor, you buy the hold. You get in after the breakout has proven itself but before the next leg, with a stop that's just below the level. It's one of the highest-probability, cleanest setups in crypto.

There are really two ways to play a breakout, and both are valid: (1) buy it as it breaks — get in the moment price clears the level, so you never miss the ones that run and don't look back; or (2) wait for the retest — let it come back to the level and buy the hold, for a tighter, better-defined risk. Each has a real trade-off, and we compare them head-to-head on the "Two Ways In" tab. This class centres on the level itself, because that's what both approaches are built on.

"The best breakouts don't look back — but the ones that do give you the lowest-risk entry of all."
— trading maxim

The psychology — why the retest happens and why it holds

A level that's been tested several times is watched by everyone. When price finally breaks it, three groups collide:

  • Trapped sellers: traders who shorted the level (or sold their coins there) are now offside. As price pushes through, their stops fire — buying that fuels the breakout.
  • Chasers & profit-takers: the breakout candle attracts momentum buyers, then a wave of quick profit-taking pushes price back down — that's the pullback that creates your retest.
  • The ones who missed: everyone who watched the breakout and didn't buy is now waiting for a second chance. They put their bids at the old level — so when price returns, real buying shows up exactly there.

That's role reversal: the old resistance becomes support because the same crowd that fought it now defends it. The hold is close to self-fulfilling — and the moment it holds, the trapped and the sidelined both pile in, sending the next leg.

How it fits your playbook: VCP = breakout from a tight coiled base. RDR = a reversal after a drop. The Pullback = buying a dip in an existing uptrend at the rising EMAs. Breakout & Retest = catching a new move the moment a key level flips from ceiling to floor. Read Bitcoin first, then pick the tool that fits the condition.

🟢 The Setup — Step by Step

🧰 What you need to run this setup (tools)

  • A charting platform (TradingView) with candlestick charts.
  • Volume turned on, ideally with a volume moving average overlay — that's how you judge a "spike" (a bar that's 2×–10× the average) at a glance.
  • The horizontal-line / rectangle tool to mark the level (and the range for the measured move).
  • Price alerts set at the level, so you're notified on the break and again on the retest — you don't have to stare at the screen.
  • Your exchange / order screen ready to place a stop-loss order the moment you're filled.
  • A position-size calculator (or the formula) so you can size the trade to your fixed risk before you enter.

✅ Setup Requirements — everything that must be true before you take the trade

  • Bitcoin / market is supportive — breakouts follow through far more often when BTC isn't falling apart.
  • A clear, obvious level — a horizontal resistance, range high, or base top that has been tested at least twice. The more obvious, the better it works (everyone sees it).
  • A real breakout close — a candle that closes decisively above the level, not just a wick poking through.
  • A real volume SPIKE on the break — the breakout candle should trade on 2×, 5×, even 10× the coin's normal volume. The bigger the spike versus the quiet base, the more real buyers are behind it. Average or below-average volume = don't trust the break.
  • A retest back to the level — price pulls back and touches the broken level, ideally on lower volume (sellers are exhausted).
  • The level holds — a bullish reaction at the level (rejection wick / bullish engulfing / a green candle that closes back up). Old resistance is now support.
  • Stop defined & tight — just below the level / below the retest reaction low. If that's too far for your risk, skip or size down.
  • Risk ≤ 1–2% of account on the trade.

The five steps

  1. Mark the level. Find a clean horizontal level a coin has bumped into repeatedly — a range high, a prior swing high, the top of a base. Draw it. This is the line the whole trade revolves around.
  2. Wait for a breakout close on a volume spike. Let price close above the level on a candle whose volume is a clear multiple of normal (2×–10×). Big volume = real demand. A wick above that closes back inside, or a break on thin volume, is a fakeout waiting to happen.
  3. Do NOT chase the breakout candle. This is the discipline. Let it run and let the profit-takers pull it back. You are waiting for the retest, not the breakout.
  4. Wait for the retest to hold. Price drifts back to the level. Watch how it behaves there: you want a bullish reaction — a rejection wick, a bullish engulfing, or a green candle that closes back up off the level. That reaction is your trigger.
  5. Buy the hold; stop below the level. Enter on the reaction, place the stop just below the broken level — tight. Because your entry is right at the level, your risk is small and your reward-to-risk is large.

Not every breakout retests. The strongest ones just keep going — that's fine, you simply don't take those (or you use a different tool like VCP to enter the base breakout itself). This setup is specifically for the breakouts that do come back, which is where the low-risk entry lives. Missing a runner costs you nothing; chasing one and getting stopped costs you real money.

⚖️ Two Ways In — Break Now vs. Retest

Same level, same volume spike — the only question is when you pull the trigger. You can buy it as it breaks, or wait and buy the retest. Neither is "correct." They're two tools with a genuine trade-off, and good traders pick based on the situation (and their own temperament).

⚡ Approach A — Buy the breakout as it breaks

Enter the moment price closes above (or trades through) the level on the volume spike.

Pros

  • You never miss the runners. The strongest breakouts explode and never retest — this is the only way to be on those.
  • You ride the full impulse from the level, capturing the fastest part of the move.
  • Simple and decisive — one rule, one trigger, no waiting or second-guessing.

Cons

  • Wider stop. Your stop (below the level / breakout-candle low) is further from entry, so each unit of reward is fewer R.
  • More fakeouts hit you. You take every bull trap; some breakouts close back below and stop you.
  • Worse average price and it can feel like chasing if the breakout candle is already large.

⏳ Approach B — Wait for the retest

Let price come back to the level and buy the hold (the reaction candle).

Pros

  • Tighter stop, bigger R. You enter right at the level, so risk is small and reward-to-risk is large.
  • Filters fakeouts. You only trade breakouts that come back and hold — the trap breakouts never trigger you.
  • Better price and a calmer, more defined decision.

Cons

  • You miss the best ones. The breakouts that run without looking back leave you behind.
  • Requires patience — and the discipline to not chase when it doesn't come back.
  • The retest can fail too — a hold is confirmation, not a guarantee.

Which one, when?

Lean "buy the break" when…

  • Bitcoin/market is strongly bullish and momentum is hot.
  • The volume spike is huge (5×–10×) — strong conviction.
  • The base is tight with little overhead resistance above.
  • It's a leader you do not want to miss.

Lean "wait for the retest" when…

  • You want tightly defined risk / the best R.
  • The breakout candle is huge/extended (chasing = far stop).
  • Conditions are choppier or the level is less clean.
  • You already missed the initial break — don't chase, wait.

The hybrid (best of both)

Take a partial position on the break so you're aboard any runner, then add on the retest if it comes back and holds. You catch the ones that fly and improve your average / add size where risk is tight. Exact entry triggers and where the stop goes for each approach are on the "Entry & Stop" tab.

🎯 Entry & Stop

Both entries revolve around the level. Pick your approach from the "Two Ways In" tab, then use the matching trigger and stop below. The one thing to get exactly right is where the stop goes — it's spelled out for each approach.

Entry — the two triggers

⚡ Buy the break (Approach A)

Your trigger is the breakout candle closing above the level on the volume spike. Enter on/just after that close. (More aggressive: enter intrabar as price pushes through the level with volume clearly surging — you accept more fakeouts to never miss a runner.)

⏳ Buy the retest (Approach B)

Wait for price to come back to the level and enter on the bullish reaction candle that holds it: a lower-wick / pin bar, a bullish engulfing, or a green candle that closes back up off the level.

Within the retest, you can be more or less patient: standard = enter when the reaction candle closes back up; aggressive = bid right at the level and buy the touch (best price, but it can slice through); conservative = wait for the reaction candle to close, then enter only if the next candle breaks its high.

Where EXACTLY to place the stop

The stop always goes on the far side of the level — the trade's whole premise is "the level now holds as support," so the stop lives where that premise is proven wrong.

If you bought the break

Stop just below the broken level (back inside the old range). If the breakout candle is very tall, you can instead use just below the breakout candle's low — whichever keeps your risk acceptable. This stop is wider (you entered above the level) — that's the cost of not missing runners.

If you bought the retest

Stop just below the low of the retest reaction candle (which sits right at the level). This is the tightest stop and the biggest reward-to-risk — the payoff for waiting.

  • Add a small buffer — don't sit exactly on the level. Place the stop a hair below the level / the candle low (a few ticks, a small %), because crypto loves to wick a level to hunt stops and snap back. Enough to survive a wick, not so much that it wrecks your risk.
  • Universal invalidation: if a full candle closes back below the level, the breakout has failed (a bull trap) — exit immediately, even if your stop price wasn't tagged. Failed breakouts often reverse hard.
  • Never widen the stop to "give it room." If you can't take the trade at the correct stop, size down or skip it.

💰 Stop example (round numbers)

Say the level is $100 and the base low was $80.

  • Retest entry: price breaks out, comes back, and the reaction candle holds at $101 with a low of $99.5. Stop just under the level at $99. Risk = $101 − $99 = $2 (~2%). Tight.
  • Break entry: you bought the breakout close at $104. Stop still goes below the level at $99. Risk = $104 − $99 = $5 (~4.8%). Same stop, wider risk — that's the exact trade-off between the two approaches, in dollars.

Position size & risk

  • Risk ≤ 1–2% of your account per trade. The tight stop lets you take a meaningful position while keeping dollar risk small.
  • Reward-to-risk is the edge. Whichever entry you use, size the position so the distance to your stop equals your fixed risk — the tighter the stop, the more size you can carry for the same dollar risk (and the more R a given move is worth).

Crypto note: only trade breakouts in the direction of the broader market — take longs when Bitcoin is stable/bullish. Because crypto loves to wick levels and hunt stops, favour the close-based confirmation (breakout close above; retest candle closes back up) over reacting to intrabar spikes, and keep any leverage well away from liquidation.

🛠️ Exits

  • Measured-move target: take the height of the range/base (from the level down to the range low) and project it up from the breakout level. That's a logical first target — trim a piece there.
  • Sell into strength: scale out into a sharp push or into the next obvious resistance / prior high, so you bank the move while buyers are eager.
  • Trail the rest: use the 9 EMA as a trailing stop in fast moves, the 21 EMA in steadier trends — exit when price closes below your chosen trail.
  • Structure stop: also exit if price loses the broken level again or breaks the trend (a clear lower high / lower low).

📏 Measured move — worked example (round numbers)

The measured move = the height of the range, projected up from the level.

  1. Range: base low = $80, resistance/level = $100.
  2. Height = $100 − $80 = $20.
  3. Project up from the level: target = $100 + $20 = $120.

Now tie it to risk. Retest entry at $101, stop at $99 → risk = $2. Reward to the $120 target = $120 − $101 = $19. That's about 9.5R — risk $1 to make $9.50. Even taking only half the target is a big multiple of your risk. (Round the numbers to whatever the coin trades at — the method is identical.)

Match the exit to the move: in a rip, take more into strength; in a grind, trail wider and give it room. The aim is to cut a failed breakout tiny and let a clean one run into a big multiple of your risk.

🔎 Filters & Confluence

  • Bitcoin / market supportive — breakouts follow through in healthy markets, fail in falling ones.
  • Obvious, multi-tested level — the more traders watching it, the stronger the reaction.
  • Breakout closes above the level — a close, not a wick.
  • Volume spikes on the break (2×–10× normal) — the single best tell that a breakout is real, not a trap.
  • Retest on lighter volume — the pullback is profit-taking, not fresh selling.
  • Bullish reaction at the level — rejection wick / engulfing / green close = the level holds.
  • Confluence bonus — a rising 9/21 EMA, a round number, or a higher-timeframe level sitting at the same price makes the hold stronger.

⚠️ The failed breakout (bull trap)

The one that costs money: price closes above the level, sucks in buyers, then closes right back below it. That's a bull trap and it often reverses hard. Your protection is built in — the stop just below the level. Take the loss without hesitation; a failed breakout is itself a signal the other way.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  1. Don't chase the breakout — buy the retest. The second chance is the low-risk chance.
  2. Real breakout = a close above the level on a volume spike (2×–10×). A wick, or thin volume, is not a breakout.
  3. Enter when the level holds as new support (bullish reaction at the level); stop just below the level — tight.
  4. Role reversal is the engine: old resistance becomes support because the crowd that watched it now defends it.
  5. Failed breakout = get out. A decisive close back below the level is a bull trap — take the small loss.
  6. Exit: measured move + sell into strength, then trail with the 9/21 EMA. Only trade breakouts with a supportive Bitcoin.
