🐕 The Red Dog Reversal

Class 31: The RDR — Counter-Trend Cashflow off the Flush
Phase 7 · Strategies & Edges · Crypto · Day-trade / 1–2 day swing

📚 What the RDR Is — and the Psychology That Makes It Work

The one-sentence definition

After a few days of selling, price gets pushed below an obvious prior low — sweeping the stops and exhausting the last sellers — and then reclaims that level. The candle that flushes below and closes back above is the entry; the stop is that candle's low. You're buying the failed breakdown and riding the snap-back.

What you actually use it for

This is a counter-trend trade for cashflow — you are catching a sharp 1 to 1½-day bounce, not marrying a position. Occasionally the flush marks a genuine change of trend and the runner becomes a multi-day swing, but you do not plan for that. You plan for the quick, violent move back up, book most of it fast, and free your capital for the next setup. Treating it as a cashflow tool — not a "maybe it's the bottom" hope trade — is what keeps the win rate and the psychology healthy.

⚠️ The trend is your friend — this is the gate, not a footnote

The RDR only works well when the broad market (Bitcoin) is bullish. In a bull tape, dips get bought, flushes get reclaimed hard, and the snap-backs are clean and forceful. In a weak or bearish tape, the same pattern fails faster, moves erratically, and lacks follow-through — flushes just keep flushing. You can still trade it when the market is weak, but you must get very tactical: smaller size, faster exits, far pickier setups. Default: hunt RDR longs when Bitcoin is in an uptrend.

The psychology — why this edge exists

The RDR works because of how humans behave around an obvious price level. Three forces collide at the prior low, and understanding them is what gives you the conviction to buy when it feels scariest.

1. The liquidity grab (the stop-hunt)

Every trader who bought near a visible support put a stop-loss just below it — that's "the obvious place" for a stop, so stops pile up in a cluster right under the prior low. Those resting stops are sell orders waiting to fire. Large players (and the market's own mechanics) push price down into that cluster, the stops trigger in a cascade, and that wave of forced selling provides the liquidity for big buyers to fill their orders cheaply. Once those stops are gone, the fuel for lower prices is gone with them. The "flush" is literally the market harvesting that liquidity.

2. Seller exhaustion (capitulation)

By the third or fourth down day, fear is at its peak and almost everyone who was going to sell has already sold. The flush below the low is the final puke — the last weak hands give up at the worst possible moment. When the new low cannot attract any more selling — when there's simply no one left to hit the bid — supply is exhausted. Price has nowhere to go but up. The reclaim is the visible proof that supply just dried up.

3. Trapped traders (the fuel for the snap-back)

The breakdown looked like the start of the next leg down, so a wave of traders shorted the break (and breakout-buyers who'd just been stopped out are sidelined and bitter). The instant price reclaims the level, every one of those shorts is offside and trapped. Their stops are now above — so as price rises, their stops trigger as buy orders, adding fuel. Meanwhile the sidelined bulls, seeing the failed breakdown, rush back in. Forced short-covering + fresh buying = the explosive reclaim. You are positioned the instant the trap springs.

Put simply: the prior low is a battleground. Bears think it's breaking and short it; bulls think it's a washout and buy it. The reclaim is the moment control flips from sellers to buyers — and that single, observable event is your entry trigger. You're not predicting the bottom; you're reacting to proof that it already happened.

Why the FIRST reclaim — and why it's uncomfortable

You enter on the first candle that flushes the prior low and closes back above it. You do not wait for a second, deeper flush to "confirm," and you do not chase after price has already run. Buying right after a scary breakdown feels wrong — which is exactly why the edge exists: most people can't pull the trigger into that fear, so those who can get the best price with the tightest risk. The discomfort is the edge.

"The market is a device for transferring money from the impatient to the patient — and from those who panic at the lows to those who buy them with a plan."
— the spirit of the RDR

Why the bounce is reliable enough to trade for cashflow

In a bull market, mean-reversion is strong: stretched moves snap back. The RDR stacks that with a stop-hunt and a short-squeeze, so the reaction is usually fast and forceful — strong reversals routinely run 5%+ in a day and close near the highs. Because the move is quick, you take profit quickly (next tab on management), book a guaranteed win on the way, and don't overstay.

🟢 The Long — Step by Step

This is the bread-and-butter trade. Walk it in order; each step has a "what you're looking for."

  1. Confirm the context (the gate). Bitcoin / broad market in an uptrend. The coin is ideally a leader (outperforming BTC). Without this, stand down or go tiny.
  2. Find a stretched, one-sided sell-off. Look for 3 down days as the ideal (1, 2 or 4 also work). The more washed-out and emotional the drop, the better the snap. RSI getting oversold is a plus.
  3. Mark the prior low (yesterday's low). This is the obvious support / swing low with stops sitting beneath it. That level is your line in the sand.
  4. Wait for the flush. Price must trade below that prior low — the stop-sweep / final washout. (No flush, no trade.)
  5. Take the entry on the reclaim. The moment price trades back above the prior low, the breakdown has failed — that candle is your entry. Buy your full position. Confirm by candle close back above the level so a wick doesn't fake you out. This is the first reclaim — don't wait for another flush.
  6. Set the stop = the low of that candle (the low of the day / flush low). Tight, logical, pre-defined.
  7. Manage the exit (see Exits tab): book 50% just below the entry candle's high, 25% at 8-EMA overextension / resistance, trail the last 25%.
  • Don't chase. If the reclaim doesn't trigger, or price is already extended above the level, skip it. There's always another setup.
  • Igniting bar = hold more. If the reclaim comes on a big, strong candle with heavy volume, plan to hold a larger piece for a bigger move.
  • Best confluence: the reclaim also takes back the 8 & 21 EMAs, and/or the flush happened right at a key support (more stops there = bigger reaction).

🔴 The Short (the mirror)

Exact mirror image — fade an overextended pop. Use sparingly, and only when the broad tape is weak (the trend gate cuts both ways).

  1. Context: market weak / rolling over; the coin is a laggard or showing exhaustion.
  2. Stretched run up — 2–4 up days, overbought, euphoric.
  3. Mark the prior high (yesterday's high) — buy-side stops and breakout orders sit just above it.
  4. Wait for the flush above it — price pokes above the prior high, trapping late longs.
  5. Entry on the reject: the candle that pops above the prior high and closes back below it is your short entry (confirm by close). Stop = that candle's high.
  6. Manage the same way, inverted.

⚠️ Shorting is the harder, riskier side

Counter-trend shorts in a bull market fail fast, and in crypto they mean perps/leverage with liquidation + funding cost (Class 19). Master the long first; only short when the broad market is clearly weak.

🎯 Entry & Stop

The entry candle (read this carefully)

  • The entry is the single candle that trades below the prior low and closes back above it — the flush-and-reclaim candle itself. You enter as it reclaims; you do not wait for the next candle.
  • It's the first reclaim after the down move — not a later, deeper flush.
  • Confirm by candle close back above the level (crypto wicks the level constantly). A close above = real reclaim.
  • Buy the full position on that reclaim. Don't chase if it's already run.

The stop

  • Stop = the low of that entry candle (the low of the day / the flush low). It's tight because the flush wick is right there.
  • Hard invalidation: if price closes below your entry on day 1, get out — don't carry a failed reclaim overnight.

Risk: never more than 2% of total capital on a single idea. The first thing you do on any trade is define where you get out. Because the stop sits right under the flush, the per-share risk is small — so you can use leverage and take a full position while still risking only 2%. The tight stop is precisely what makes sizing up safe. If the stop is too far for 2%, the trade is too big — pass or size down.

Tooling: since you scale out in pieces, keep a weighted-average calculator open to track your true blended entry and net P&L across tiers.

🛠️ Exits & Trade Management

Most RDRs that work pay fast, and a good number fail — so bank profit quickly and make the rest prove itself.

The profit ladder

  1. Always book 50% on day 1 — just below the previous candle's high. The "previous candle" is your entry candle: you entered near its low, so you take half off just under its high. This guarantees you bank a gain before risking the rest.
  2. Take another 25% when price is overextended from the 8 EMA OR it's about to test an important resistance.
  3. Trail the last 25% — raise the stop to each new pivot / the 8–21 EMA until you're stopped or you choose to exit.

Why 50% first (the psychology): taking half is a win/win. If you're right, you locked in half the move; if you're wrong, you were only wrong on half. Booking just 30% and being wrong on 70% is psychologically corrosive — it makes you hesitant and bitter. The rule protects your mindset as much as your capital.

Holding for a swing

  • Igniting bar on heavy volume → hold more; the move may be larger.
  • Take ⅓ home overnight only if it closes strong; otherwise take it off. Always take the bulk intraday on day 1.
  • Day 2: take another piece into strength; trail the remainder with new pivots and the 8/21 EMA.
  • Not convincing you of strength? Take 50% (or all) before the close.

When the reclaim fails

If price loses the level again / closes below your entry, exit at the stop — a small, pre-defined loss. Never widen the stop. The edge is that winners pay multiples of the small losers; protect the "small."

🔎 Filters, Confluence & How to Find Them

Quality filters — stack these

  • Bull market / Bitcoin uptrend — the non-negotiable gate.
  • Leader, outperforming BTC — hunt RDR pullbacks in names showing strength vs Bitcoin (check the /BTC pair).
  • High volatility / ATR — enough range to move fast and get in/out the same day (frees capital).
  • Real liquidity — liquid names only; thin coins fake the level and slip fills.
  • At a key level — best when the flush also breaks an obvious support/resistance (more stops = bigger reaction).
  • RSI stretched — oversold (long) / overbought (short).
  • Near the EMAs — price by the 8/21 EMA gives a clean level to trade against. Rule of thumb: above the 8/21 EMA → portfolio approach (lean long); below them → get tactical.

Indicators to run

8 & 21 EMA (core — support/resistance + trail), 50 & 200 EMA (bigger-picture trend), and VWAP for intraday (test it on your timeframe). Intraday: the 5 / 15 / 30-minute charts; daily/weekly for the important levels and swings.

How to find them (scanning & alerts)

Hunt at the inflection points, not in the middle of nowhere. Set price alerts at important daily/weekly levels — that's where the volatility and volume show up and where RDRs actually fire. This keeps you present only at the moments that matter instead of staring at charts for hours. (This alone may be most of what you need.)

  • Volume-spike alert: a sudden, strong surge in volume = a breakout/breakdown — it pulls your eye to the chart, where you'll find the big candles an RDR needs.
  • Read the tape: if everything's breaking out, then after 3–4 days actively hunt the counter-trend RDR.
  • Timing: RDRs usually appear on the 3rd or 4th day of a run/drop — be on alert from day 3, and stay alert the whole session (they often fire in the afternoon after a level breaks).

⭐ The Hybrid — LOD + Moving-Average Confluence

The highest-conviction version. Statistically, the strongest snap-backs happen in the narrow band between the low of the day (after 3+ down days) and a moving average sitting just below as support. When price flushes into that zone you get the RDR reclaim and MA support firing together.

How to tier it

  1. Tier 1 — feeler at the MA. After 3+ down days, if price drops into the MA support, take a small starter right at the MA with a tight stop.
  2. Tier 2 — add on the RDR. When price flushes and reclaims the prior low (the entry candle), add the rest.
  3. Let it run, then take ½ back to your Tier 1 size as it pays.
  4. Let the Tier 1 runner ride 2–3 days, trailing stops up.

The A+++ trade: when the gap between the low of the day and the MA is small and the upside is large, hit it hard — risk is tiny relative to the move. Tier 3 is on the table here too: with the blended price, the risk still works because your invalidation (under the MA / flush) is so close.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  1. Counter-trend cashflow trade (~1–1½ days). Flush a prior level, reclaim it, fade the trapped traders, book it fast.
  2. The trend is your friend — only press it when Bitcoin is bullish; it fails fast and gets erratic otherwise.
  3. The entry is the ONE candle that flushes the prior low and closes back above it (the first reclaim). Stop = that candle's low. Confirm by close, don't chase.
  4. The edge is psychological: a stop-hunt + seller exhaustion + trapped shorts forced to cover, all firing at the level the instant it reclaims.
  5. Risk ≤ 2%, leverage allowed because the stop is tight. Out if it closes below entry on day 1.
  6. Exits: 50% just below the entry candle's high → 25% at 8-EMA overextension / resistance → trail 25%.
  7. The hybrid (LOD + MA) is the A+++ version: feeler at the MA, add on the reclaim, hit it hard when the gap is tight.
