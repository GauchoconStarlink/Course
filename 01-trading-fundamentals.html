Trading vs Investing

Let us clear up a common confusion right away: trading and investing are not the same thing.

Investing - long-term wealth building

  • Timeframe: months to years
  • Goal: long-term asset appreciation
  • Approach: buy and hold quality assets
  • Focus: fundamental value and future potential
  • Decision: "Will this be worth more in five years?"
  • Crypto example: buying Bitcoin and holding it for the long run (HODLing)

Trading - short-term profit opportunities

  • Timeframe: minutes to weeks
  • Goal: profit from price movements
  • Approach: active buying and selling
  • Focus: price action and technical patterns
  • Decision: "Will this move up or down soon?"
  • Crypto example: buying BTC and selling it a few percent higher a few days later

Both approaches are valid, and you can do both. Many people in crypto hold some coins long-term while trading with a separate portion of their portfolio.

"If you aren't willing to own a stock for ten years, don't even think about owning it for ten minutes."
- Warren Buffett (on investing, not trading)

Key distinctions

  • Time commitment. Investing: minimal, check in occasionally. Trading: significant, you monitor regularly.
  • Risk profile. Investing: lower risk, gradual growth. Trading: higher risk, faster results.
  • Skills required. Investing: research and patience. Trading: technical analysis and discipline.

Markets You Can Trade

Trading is not limited to one market. These are the main options.

Stock market

Shares of companies such as Apple, Tesla, or Microsoft.

Forex

Currency pairs such as EUR/USD or GBP/USD.

Commodities

Raw materials such as gold, oil, or wheat.

Crypto

Digital assets such as Bitcoin, Ethereum, and altcoins.

Futures

Contracts for future delivery. Allow leverage and shorting. More advanced.

Options

Rights to buy (calls) or sell (puts) at a set price. Complex, for later.

Futures are contracts to buy or sell an asset at a set price on a future date, popular in crypto and commodities. Options give the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell at a specific price. Both are advanced instruments we cover in later classes.

How Price Actually Moves

Before any pattern or indicator, understand the one thing underneath all of them: a price is simply the last agreed transaction between a buyer and a seller. It moves for one reason only, supply and demand.

Every trade has two sides. For you to buy, someone has to sell to you. For you to sell, someone has to buy from you. Price is the point where a buyer and a seller agree, right now. When buyers are more aggressive than sellers, price ticks up. When sellers are more aggressive, it ticks down. That is the entire engine.

Supply and demand in plain terms

  • More demand than supply (eager buyers, few willing sellers) pushes price up.
  • More supply than demand (eager sellers, few willing buyers) pushes price down.
  • Balance (buyers and sellers roughly equal) makes price drift sideways in a range.

News, sentiment, and big players all matter, but only because of how they change supply and demand. A headline does not move price directly. It moves price by making buyers or sellers act.

The market moves in cycles

Markets do not go straight up or straight down. They rotate through four repeating phases. Learning to recognize which phase you are in tells you which tool to reach for.

1. Accumulation

After a long decline, price goes quiet and sideways. Informed buyers quietly build positions while everyone else is fearful. Hard to spot in real time.

2. Markup

The uptrend. Higher highs and higher lows. This is where trend and breakout traders make the easiest money, and where most of this course lives.

3. Distribution

Price stalls near the top and goes sideways again. Informed sellers hand their positions to late, excited buyers. Momentum fades.

4. Markdown

The downtrend. Lower highs and lower lows. Latecomers sell in fear, and the cycle resets for the next accumulation.

Why this matters for you: the same setup behaves differently in each phase. A breakout during a markup tends to run. The same breakout during a markdown tends to fail. Read the phase first, then choose your trade.

What each phase feels like

  • Accumulation: the news is still bad and most people are bored or fearful. Quiet and sideways, easy to ignore. This is where patient money gets positioned.
  • Markup: the trend is clear, headlines turn positive, and confidence builds. The easiest phase to make money, and the one this course spends the most time on.
  • Distribution: euphoria and "this only goes up" talk. Price stops making clean new highs and starts to chop. Informed money quietly sells to the crowd.
  • Markdown: fear takes over, every bounce gets sold, and the crowd capitulates near the lows, right before the next accumulation begins.

Cycles repeat inside cycles. The same four phases show up on the weekly chart, the daily, and the hourly. A markup on the daily can contain several small accumulation-to-markdown cycles on the hourly. You do not need to call the exact top or bottom. You just need to know roughly where you are, and trade with the phase rather than against it.

"The four most dangerous words in investing are: this time it's different."

Trading Styles and Timeframes

Not all trading is the same. The timeframe you choose changes your approach, your time commitment, and your lifestyle. There are four main styles.

Minutes

Scalping

Many trades per session, positions held for minutes. Extreme screen time and the highest stress of any style. Not recommended for beginners.

Example: buy BTC, sell about 0.7% higher two hours later, many times over.

Intraday

Day Trading

Positions opened and closed the same day, never held overnight. High time commitment and constant chart monitoring.

Example: buy BTC in the morning, sell around 2% higher before the day closes, no overnight hold.

Days to weeks

Swing Trading

Hold for several days on the daily and 4H charts. Check charts two or three times a day. Manageable alongside a job.

Example: buy BTC, sell 9 to 10% higher after one week.

Best for most beginners
Weeks to months

Position Trading

Hold for extended periods on the weekly and daily charts. Low time commitment. This is the closest style to investing.

Example: buy near a bear-market bottom, hold for months, exit far higher up the trend.

Trading strategies - different approaches to the market

Separate from your style is your strategy, which depends on the market condition:

  • Trend trading. "The trend is your friend." Trade in the direction of the prevailing trend. The most popular and often most reliable approach. Example: BTC is in an uptrend, so you buy dips and ride higher.
  • Breakout trading. "Catch the explosion." Trade when price breaks out of a range or a key level. Can catch the start of major moves, but carries the risk of false breakouts. Example: BTC consolidates for weeks, then you buy the break above the range high.
  • Reversal trading. "Catch the turn before everyone else." Trade against the prevailing trend to catch a reversal. Higher risk, but it can catch major moves early. Example: BTC has fallen for weeks, you spot reversal signals and buy the turn.
"Don't focus on making money; focus on protecting what you have."
- Paul Tudor Jones, billionaire trader

Choosing your style

Choose the style that fits the life you actually have, not the one that sounds most exciting.

  • Scalping only if you can sit at the screen for hours, thrive under intense pressure, and have iron emotional control. Not for beginners.
  • Day trading if you can dedicate most of the day, make quick decisions, and are not working full-time.
  • Swing trading if you work full-time, want work-life balance, prefer thoughtful decisions, and can check charts two or three times a day. This is the best fit for most beginners.
  • Position trading if you have very limited time, are patient, and prefer big-picture thinking with minimal stress.

Be honest about your available time. If you have a full-time job and cannot watch charts constantly, scalping or day trading will be disastrous. Match the style to your schedule and you remove a huge source of failure before you even start.

Long vs Short: Trading Both Directions

One of the most important concepts in trading: you can make money when prices go up (going long) and when prices go down (going short).

Why this matters: most beginners only think about buying low and selling high. Professional traders can profit in any market condition, bull or bear.

Going long (buying)

Buying an asset because you expect the price to rise.

  • Buy first, sell later
  • Profit when price goes up
  • Risk is capped: you can only lose 100%
  • Upside is theoretically unlimited

Example: buy BTC, it rises about 12%, you sell for a gain.

Going short (selling)

Selling an asset you do not own because you expect the price to fall.

  • Sell first (borrow), buy back later
  • Profit when price goes down
  • Gain is capped at 100% (price can only reach zero)
  • Loss is theoretically unlimited (price can rise forever)

Example: short BTC, it falls about 12%, you buy back lower for a gain.

Why shorting is riskier than going long

When you are long, the worst case is a total loss of what you put in, down 100%. When you are short, the loss is open-ended, because a price can rise 200%, 500%, or more, and short squeezes can force everyone to buy back at once and spike it even higher. Historically, markets and especially crypto trend upward over the long run, so shorting also means fighting the long-term current.

"It's not whether you're right or wrong that's important, but how much money you make when you're right and how much you lose when you're wrong."
- George Soros

Beginner recommendation: learn to go long first. Get consistently profitable with longs for several months before you attempt a single short. Shorting is an advanced skill, not a starting point.

Anatomy of a Trade

Every trade, no matter the strategy, has the same skeleton. Learn it once here and every later class will click into place.

  1. The idea. A reason to be in the trade: a setup, a level, a pattern. No reason, no trade.
  2. The entry. The price where you actually get in.
  3. The stop loss. The price that proves you wrong, decided before you enter. This is the single most important number in the whole trade.
  4. The target. Where you plan to take profit, ideally at a level that makes sense on the chart.
  5. The position size. How much you buy, chosen so that if the stop hits, you only lose a small, fixed slice of your account.
  6. The exit. Price hits your target (a win), hits your stop (a small loss), or you manage it along the way. Either way, you knew the outcomes before you clicked buy.

One trade, start to finish

Say Bitcoin pulls back to a support level you have marked, in an uptrend.

  • Idea: price is bouncing off support while the trend is up.
  • Entry: you buy at the level.
  • Stop: if price closes about 3% below the level, your idea is wrong, so that is your stop. Call that distance 1R, your risk.
  • Target: the prior high, about 9% up. That is 3R, three times your risk.
  • Size: you size the position so that if the stop hits, you lose only 1 to 2% of your account.
  • Exit: price runs to the target and you sell for a 3R win. Had it hit the stop first, you take the small 1R loss and move on with no drama.

The one habit that separates winners: the stop and the size are decided before you enter, never after. Amateurs enter first and figure out the risk once it is already hurting. You will do the opposite, every single time.

Trader's Vocabulary

The words you will hear in every class and every trading room. Keep this tab bookmarked and come back to it whenever a term trips you up.

Price and orders
BidThe highest price a buyer is currently willing to pay.
Ask (offer)The lowest price a seller is currently willing to accept.
SpreadThe gap between the bid and the ask. A tighter spread means a more liquid market.
Market orderBuy or sell right now at the best available price. Fast, but you accept the current price.
Limit orderBuy or sell only at a price you choose or better. You wait for the market to come to you.
SlippageThe difference between the price you expected and the price you actually got.
Market activity
VolumeHow much of an asset traded in a period. Confirms whether a move has real participation behind it.
LiquidityHow easily you can buy or sell without moving the price. Major coins are liquid; tiny coins are not.
VolatilityHow much and how fast price moves. Crypto is highly volatile.
Order bookThe live list of all bids and asks waiting to be filled.
Positions
LongA position that profits when price rises. You bought.
ShortA position that profits when price falls. You sold borrowed units first.
LeverageBorrowed size that multiplies both gains and losses. Powerful and dangerous. Covered fully in Class 19.
MarginThe collateral you put up to open a leveraged position.
LiquidationWhen a leveraged position is force-closed because losses ate your margin. To be avoided.
Position sizeHow much you commit to a single trade, set by your risk, not your excitement.
Outcomes and risk
P&LProfit and loss. Realized once you close the trade; unrealized while it is still open.
DrawdownThe drop from your account's peak to its low point. How far you are down from the top.
R-multipleYour result measured in units of risk. Risk 1R to make 3R is a 3 to 1 trade.
Risk to rewardHow much you stand to make versus how much you risk. The heart of every good trade.
Market conditions
Bull marketA sustained uptrend. Optimism and higher highs.
Bear marketA sustained downtrend. Fear and lower lows.
TrendThe general direction of price: up, down, or sideways.
ConsolidationA pause where price moves sideways in a range before its next move.

Reality Check: The Truth About Trading

I need to be honest with you. Most people who try trading lose money. I am not telling you this to scare you off. I am telling you because the traders who make it are the ones who took this seriously from day one, and I want you to be one of them.

"The hard reality is that every trader has to lose, and it's going to hurt. The key is to lose as little as possible while you're learning to win."
- Mark Douglas, trading psychology expert

Why most traders fail, and what the winners do

Why most fail

  • Jump in with no education
  • Risk too much per trade
  • Let emotions drive decisions
  • Chase quick profits
  • Trade with no plan
  • Quit after the first losses

What winners do

  • Commit to education first
  • Manage risk on every trade
  • Stay disciplined and control emotion
  • Focus on a consistent process
  • Follow a tested strategy
  • Learn from every single trade

Your advantage: you are here, learning, before risking real money. That already puts you ahead of most people, who jump in blindly and pay for the lesson.

A realistic timeline

  • Months 1 to 3: learn the fundamentals, understand the concepts, paper trade, build confidence.
  • Months 4 to 6: small real trades, feel real emotions, refine your strategy, learn from mistakes.
  • Months 6 to 12: develop consistency, size up gradually, find your personal style, build a track record.

Key Takeaways

Four ideas to carry out of this class:

  1. Trading is not investing. Trading works shorter timeframes to capitalize on price movement; investing builds wealth over the long run. Different goals, skills, and time commitments. You can do both.
  2. You can profit in any market direction. Long profits from prices rising, short profits from prices falling. Pros use both, but beginners should master longs first.
  3. Choose a style that fits your life. There are four: scalping, day trading, swing trading, and position trading. Do not fight your schedule. Swing trading suits most beginners with a job.
  4. This is a learnable skill, but it takes time. Most people fail because they skip education and risk management. Success comes from education, practice, discipline, and patience.
