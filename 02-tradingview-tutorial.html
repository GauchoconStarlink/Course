1. Daily & Weekly Timeframes

Why Daily (1D) and Weekly (1W)?

Daily Chart (1D):

  • Each candle = 1 day of price action
  • Shows medium-term trends and structure
  • Filters out intraday noise
  • Perfect for swing trading (our focus)
  • Provides clear support/resistance levels

Weekly Chart (1W):

  • Each candle = 1 week of price action
  • Shows big-picture trends
  • Identifies major support/resistance zones
  • Great for context and bias (bullish/bearish)
  • Less false signals

Pro Workflow

Start on the Weekly chart to get the big picture and identify the overall trend. Then zoom into the Daily chart for entry/exit points. This top-down approach keeps you aligned with the major trend.

2. EMA 200 & EMA 50

Exponential Moving Averages

What are EMAs?

Moving averages smooth out price data to show the average price over a specific period. The EMA (Exponential) gives more weight to recent prices, making it more responsive than a Simple Moving Average (SMA).

EMA 200 (The Trend Filter):

  • The 200-period EMA is the most watched indicator globally
  • Price above EMA 200 = Bullish bias
  • Price below EMA 200 = Bearish bias
  • Often acts as dynamic support in uptrends
  • Often acts as dynamic resistance in downtrends
"My metric for everything I look at is the 200-day moving average of closing prices. I've seen too many things go to zero, stocks and commodities. The whole trick in investing is: 'How do I keep from losing everything?' If you use the 200-day moving average rule, then you get out. You play defense, and you get out."
- Paul Tudor Jones

EMA 50 (The Momentum Line):

  • More responsive to recent price changes
  • Shows short-term momentum direction
  • When EMA 50 crosses above/below EMA 200, it signals major trend changes
  • Can act as support/resistance in strong trends
How to Add EMAs:
Full step-by-step setup is in the Chart Setup tab.

Tip: Change the color of each EMA so you can distinguish them easily (I use pink for 50, yellow for 200, matching the screenshots in this lesson)

Indicators Menu: Click "Indicators" button in top toolbar to open this search panel. Type "EMA" to find Moving Average Exponential.

EMA Settings: Change "Length" to 200 for EMA 200 (or 50 for EMA 50). Click "Style" tab to change color.

Chart with EMAs: Yellow line is EMA 200, pink/magenta line is EMA 50. Both smoothly follow price action.

3. Fibonacci Retracements

Finding Potential Reversal Levels

What is Fibonacci?

Fibonacci retracement levels (23.6%, 38.2%, 50%, 61.8%, 78.6%) are horizontal lines that indicate potential support/resistance areas based on the Fibonacci sequence. Markets often retrace to these levels before continuing the trend. We dedicate a full class to Fibonacci later in the course.

Why We Use It:

  • Identifies high-probability entry points in trending markets
  • Shows where price might bounce or reverse
  • Key levels: 38.2%, 50%, 61.8% (most important)
  • Combines well with support/resistance analysis
  • Self-fulfilling prophecy - traders watch these levels

How to Use Fibonacci:

  • Draw from significant low to high (in uptrends)
  • Draw from significant high to low (in downtrends)
  • Look for price reactions at 38.2%, 50%, or 61.8%
  • Combine with other support/resistance for confirmation
How to Draw Fibonacci:
1. Select Fibonacci Retracement tool (left sidebar)
2. In an uptrend: Click on the swing low, drag to swing high
3. In a downtrend: Click on the swing high, drag to swing low
4. Watch for price reactions at the key levels

Key levels to watch: 38.2% (shallow retracement), 50% (midpoint), 61.8% (golden ratio - deepest healthy retracement)

Fibonacci Retracement: Horizontal lines show key levels (0%, 23.6%, 38.2%, 50%, 61.8%, 78.6%, 100%). Watch for price reactions at 38.2%, 50%, and 61.8%.

4. Volume & Volume Delta

Understanding Market Participation

Volume:

  • Shows how much of an asset was traded in each time period
  • Higher volume = More conviction behind price moves
  • Lower volume = Weak moves, easily reversed
  • Volume should confirm price action
  • Breakouts with high volume are more reliable

Volume Delta (Advanced):

  • Shows the difference between buying and selling volume
  • Green/positive = More buying pressure
  • Red/negative = More selling pressure
  • Helps identify who's in control (buyers or sellers)
  • Useful for spotting divergences

Note on Volume Delta

Volume Delta is typically available through premium indicators or specific exchanges. The standard volume bar (included free on TradingView) is sufficient for most analysis. Volume Delta adds extra confirmation but isn't required. Volume Delta gets full coverage in our Technical Indicators class.

Volume is already displayed by default at the bottom of your chart!

Volume bars appear below the price chart automatically. Green bars = price closed higher than it opened. Red bars = price closed lower.

Volume Bars: Green bars = bullish days (price closed up), Red bars = bearish days (price closed down). Tall bars = high volume, short bars = low volume.

5. Horizontal Lines - Your Key Levels

Marking Support, Resistance & Pivot Points

Why Horizontal Lines Are Essential:

  • Mark key support and resistance levels
  • Identify pivot points (areas where price reversed)
  • Set entry, stop loss, and take profit levels
  • Simple but incredibly powerful
  • Most professional traders use these daily

What I Mark:

  • Previous highs/lows: Major swing points
  • Round numbers: $100,000, $110,000, $120,000 (psychological levels)
  • Key pivot points: Where price reversed strongly
  • Current range boundaries: Top and bottom of consolidation
How to Draw Horizontal Lines:
1. Select "Horizontal Line" tool (left sidebar)
2. Click on a price level where you see significance
3. Add text labels to remember why it's important
4. Use different colors for different types (support = green, resistance = red)

Hotkey: Alt+H (Windows) or Option+H (Mac) for quick access

Horizontal Line: Simple horizontal line marking a key level. Add text labels ("TEXT" shown here) to remember why this level is significant.

My System for Pivot Points

I mark pivot points with horizontal lines AND add text notes explaining the significance (e.g., "Strong support - held 3 times" or "Previous ATH - major resistance"). This creates a visual map of important levels I can reference quickly.

Save Your Layout

Supplementary Tools

These tools I use from time to time for specific situations or additional confirmation. Not essential for daily analysis, but valuable when needed.

ATR - Average True Range

Measuring Volatility

What is ATR?

ATR measures how much an asset typically moves over a given period. It shows the average distance between the high and low of each candle, giving you a sense of volatility. ATR returns in depth in our Technical Indicators and Stop Loss classes.

Why I Use It:

  • Setting stop losses: Place stops beyond the ATR range to avoid getting stopped out by normal volatility
  • Position sizing: Higher ATR = more risk, so reduce position size
  • Profit targets: Estimate realistic price moves based on ATR
  • Market conditions: Rising ATR = increasing volatility, falling ATR = consolidation

When I Use It:

  • Before entering a trade (to set appropriate stop loss)
  • When volatility seems unusual (to confirm)
  • For calculating position size based on risk
  • Not needed for every single analysis
How to Add ATR:
1. Click "Indicators"
2. Search "ATR"
3. Select "Average True Range"
4. Default setting (14 periods) works well
5. ATR appears in a separate panel below the chart

Interpretation: Higher ATR value = more volatility, Lower ATR = less volatility

ATR Indicator: Appears in a separate panel below your chart. Red line shows volatility - when rising, market is more volatile; when falling, less volatile. Current ATR value shown in top left (4,107.73).

ATR for Stop Loss Placement

A common rule: Place your stop loss at 1.5x to 2x ATR away from your entry. This gives the trade room to breathe without getting stopped out by normal price fluctuation. For example, if ATR is $1,000, place stop $1,500-$2,000 away.

RSI - Relative Strength Index

Measuring Momentum

What is RSI?

RSI is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and magnitude of price movements. It oscillates between 0 and 100, helping identify overbought and oversold conditions. RSI gets full coverage in our Technical Indicators class.

Why I Use It:

  • Overbought/Oversold: RSI above 70 = potentially overbought, RSI below 30 = potentially oversold
  • Divergence: When price makes new high/low but RSI doesn't = potential reversal
  • Trend confirmation: In strong trends, RSI stays elevated/depressed
  • Entry timing: Wait for RSI pullback in trending markets

When I Use It:

  • Looking for reversal signals (divergence)
  • Confirming if a trend is overextended
  • Additional confirmation for entries/exits
  • NOT as a standalone signal (always combine with price action)

Common RSI Mistake

Don't blindly buy at RSI 30 or sell at RSI 70! In strong trends, RSI can stay overbought/oversold for extended periods. Always look at the bigger picture and trend direction first.

How to Add RSI:
1. Click "Indicators"
2. Search "RSI"
3. Select "Relative Strength Index"
4. Default setting (14 periods) is standard
5. RSI appears in a separate panel below the chart

Key levels: 70 (overbought), 30 (oversold), 50 (neutral/midpoint)

RSI Indicator: Purple line oscillates between 0-100. Reference lines at 70 (overbought), 50 (neutral), and 30 (oversold). Current RSI value shown in top left (54.02). Yellow line is RSI moving average.

RSI Divergence - Powerful Signal

Bullish Divergence: Price makes lower low, but RSI makes higher low → potential reversal up
Bearish Divergence: Price makes higher high, but RSI makes lower high → potential reversal down

Divergences are one of the most reliable RSI signals, especially on daily/weekly timeframes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions students ask about TradingView:

Q: Do I need TradingView Pro, or is the free version enough?
A: The free version is absolutely sufficient for learning and even for serious trading. Free gives you all essential features: unlimited indicators, drawing tools, and chart access.

Pro benefits (if you upgrade later): More price alerts, multiple charts on one screen, faster data updates, volume profile. But start with free - you won't feel limited.
Q: My chart looks different from yours. How do I fix it?
A: TradingView is highly customizable. Right-click on your chart → "Settings" → Adjust appearance, colors, and layout to match. The important part is having the same indicators (EMA 50, EMA 200, Volume) - visual appearance is personal preference.
Q: Why do you focus on Daily and Weekly charts? What about lower timeframes?
A: Lower timeframes (1-hour, 15-min, 5-min) have more noise and false signals. Daily/Weekly charts filter out this noise and show clearer trends. As a beginner, you'll make better decisions on higher timeframes. Once you're profitable on daily charts, you can explore lower timeframes for day trading.
Q: Can I use TradingView on my phone/tablet?
A: Yes! TradingView has mobile apps for iOS and Android. However, serious analysis is best done on a computer with a larger screen. Use mobile for quick checks and monitoring alerts, but do your deep analysis on desktop.
Q: How do I save my chart so I don't lose my drawings?
A: TradingView auto-saves your charts if you're logged in! All your drawings, indicators, and layouts are automatically saved to your account. You can also manually save layouts: Click "Layouts" (top right) → "Save As" → Name it.
Q: What's the difference between EMA and SMA?
A: SMA (Simple Moving Average) gives equal weight to all prices in the period. EMA (Exponential Moving Average) gives more weight to recent prices, making it more responsive to current market conditions. We use EMA because it reacts faster to price changes, which is important in volatile crypto markets.
Q: Should I use the same setup for all cryptocurrencies?
A: Yes! The same indicators work across all assets. EMA 200/50, Fibonacci, support/resistance - these are universal. You might adjust timeframes (some low-volume altcoins need higher timeframes), but the core setup remains the same.
Q: Why don't my EMAs match yours exactly?
A: Make sure you're on the same timeframe (Daily or Weekly) and using the same length (50 and 200). Also check that you selected "EMA" not "SMA". Small differences might also occur if you're viewing different exchanges (Binance vs Coinbase) - pick one exchange and stick with it for consistency.
Q: How many indicators should I use?
A: LESS IS MORE. Beginners often clutter charts with 10+ indicators, causing confusion. Our setup (EMA 50, EMA 200, Volume, occasional RSI/ATR) is intentionally minimal. Master these before adding more. Professional traders use fewer indicators than beginners, not more.
Q: Can I backtest strategies on TradingView?
A: Yes, but it requires learning Pine Script (TradingView's programming language) which is advanced. For now, you can manually backtest by scrolling back in time on the chart, identifying your entry signals, and seeing how trades would have played out. For this course, manual backtesting - scrolling back in time, spotting your entry signals, and seeing how trades would have played out - is all you need.
Q: What if I accidentally delete all my drawings?
A: Press Ctrl+Z immediately to undo! If you've already closed the chart, unfortunately TradingView doesn't have a "restore deleted drawings" feature. This is why it's important to: 1) Lock important drawings (right-click → Lock), 2) Be careful when deleting, 3) Save chart layouts regularly.
Q: Should I draw Fibonacci on every swing?
A: No! Only draw Fibonacci on significant swings - major highs to major lows or vice versa. Not every small wiggle needs a Fib. Look for swing points that represent meaningful trends (several weeks/months). Too many Fibs creates clutter and confusion.
