🔍 What Is a VCP?

A Volatility Contraction Pattern is a consolidation (a "base") where price swings get progressively smaller from left to right, while volume dries up — until the stock is coiled tight and ready to break out. Think of a spring being compressed: the tighter it gets, the more explosive the release.

Minervini's key insight Minervini: The VCP is the effect, not the cause. The pattern works because you are literally watching supply and demand resolve in real time. As each swing contracts on lighter volume, sellers are being exhausted and shares move from weak hands to strong hands (institutions). When supply stops coming to market, the next buyer has to lift the price — that's the breakout.

The "shakeout"

Within the base, price often dips just below a prior swing low, triggering the stop-losses of earlier buyers, then quickly recovers. Minervini calls this a shakeout — weak holders get "shaken out," leaving stronger hands in control. A base showing one or more clean shakeouts is generally higher probability, not lower.

What it looks like

  • Left side: larger, sharper price swings (more fear/volatility).
  • Moving right: each pullback is shallower than the last.
  • Right side: very tight, quiet price action on very low volume.
  • The release: price breaks out through the top of the final contraction on a surge of volume.
"You want to get on board when institutional money is pouring into a stock and lifting it significantly higher."
— Mark Minervini, Trade Like a Stock Market Wizard

Connects to what you already know: a VCP isn't a brand-new shape — it's a lens. A cup-with-handle, a flat base, a double bottom, an ascending triangle — any of them can be a VCP if the swings contract and volume dries up. You're already equipped: this builds directly on support & resistance (Class 4), chart patterns (Classes 6–7), volume (Class 14), and breakouts (Class 1).

🔢 The Footprint & Counting Contractions

Minervini uses a shorthand "technical footprint" to describe any VCP in one line — so you can compare bases objectively instead of eyeballing.

Example footprint:   19W   19/7   4T
  • 19W — the base is 19 weeks long.
  • 19/7 — the largest contraction was ~19% deep; the smallest (final) was ~7%.
  • 4T — there were 4 contractions in total.

Note: the number before "T" is the count of contractions. (You'll sometimes hear "T = transitions" online — that specific wording isn't confirmed in Minervini's books, so just read it as the contraction count.) commonly cited

How many contractions?

CountRead
2–6Typical range Minervini describes. Minervini
3–4The usual "sweet spot" for a clean, mature base. commonly cited
1 (sometimes 2)Often too little basing — less reliable. commonly cited
>6 / dragging onIndecision, stock going "dead," momentum fading. commonly cited

How each contraction tightens

This is the heart of the pattern: each successive contraction is roughly half the depth of the one before it. In Minervini's own words from an interview: Minervini

"The first correction might be 20%, 25%, 33%, and then it'll contract — usually the contractions are about half of the previous correction. So maybe it contracts to 10 or 15, and then contracts to 3, 4, 5 or 8%."
— Mark Minervini (interview)

So a textbook progression looks like:

25%  →  15%  →  8%   (each ≈ half the prior)

What the shrinking percentages mean: volatility and available supply are drying up. Sellers are being used up while bigger buyers quietly accumulate. By the final, tight contraction, there's almost no one left to sell — the stock is coiled.

⚠️ The contractions MUST get smaller

If a later pullback is deeper than an earlier one (e.g., 12% → 15% → 8%), it's not a valid VCP — the volatility didn't actually contract in sequence. Minervini's spirit here: if you have to talk yourself into why it's a VCP, it probably isn't one.

Depth & duration

  • Overall base depth and length vary — that's exactly why the footprint records them rather than fixing them. Minervini
  • A common generalization is ~6–12 weeks with 3–4 contractions, but treat duration as flexible, not a hard rule. commonly cited
  • Forms too fast (< ~3 weeks) → little real accumulation. Too long with too many contractions → loss of momentum. commonly cited

📉➡️📈 Volume & the Pivot Point

Volume must dry up

Price tightening alone isn't enough — volume has to contract with it. Each pullback should come on lighter volume than the last, and the final, tightest contraction should show extremely low volume — so quiet you'd almost think trading had stopped. That dry-up is the tell that sellers are exhausted. Minervini

The pivot point = the "line of least resistance"

The pivot is the high of the final, tightest contraction. Minervini (borrowing Jesse Livermore's phrase) calls this the line of least resistance — the price level that "calls for action." Above it, there's almost no overhead supply, so the path of least resistance is up.

Definition Minervini: "A proper pivot point indicates the fulfillment of a stock's consolidation and the threshold of its next advance… the optimal buy point, or the line of least resistance."

The entry trigger

  1. Price breaks up through the pivot (the high of the final contraction).
  2. Volume surges well above average on the breakout — this confirms institutional demand has overwhelmed supply. A breakout on weak volume is suspect.
  3. You buy into the breakout, near the pivot — "on the way up."

The popular thresholds — "breakout volume ~40–50%+ above average," and "enter within ~5% of the pivot" — are commonly cited guidelines, not numbers Minervini states verbatim. Use them as sensible defaults, not gospel.

"To compound your money, and not your mistakes, your goal is to buy on the way up — not on the way down."
— Mark Minervini, Think & Trade Like a Champion

Don't chase

The entry is the pivot — not several percent above it. If you miss the buy zone and the stock is already extended, let it go or wait for the next setup. Chasing an extended breakout wrecks your risk-to-reward because your stop is now far away.

After the breakout commonly cited: a healthy move should hold above its short-term moving average (e.g., the 20-day) and not close back inside the base. If it immediately loosens up and falls back below the pivot, the breakout is failing.

🧱 The Context: SEPA & the Trend Template

A VCP is only worth trading in the right context. Minervini never trades the pattern in isolation — it's the entry piece of his bigger system, SEPA.

SEPA — Specific Entry Point Analysis Minervini

1. Trend

  • Only buy stocks already in a confirmed uptrend (Stage 2). Trend comes first.

2. Fundamentals

  • Strong, accelerating earnings & sales; a real reason institutions want it.

3. Catalyst

  • A reason for the move now — new product, earnings surprise, industry tailwind.

4. Entry Point

  • This is the VCP — the precise, low-risk pivot.

5. Exit Point

  • Pre-defined stop (loss) and profit-taking plan before you enter.

The Trend Template — the 8 gates for a Stage 2 uptrend Minervini

Before a VCP "counts," the stock should pass Minervini's Trend Template (from Think & Trade Like a Champion):

  1. Price is above both the 150-day and 200-day moving averages.
  2. The 150-day MA is above the 200-day MA.
  3. The 200-day MA is trending up (at least ~1 month; ideally several).
  4. The 50-day MA is above both the 150-day and 200-day MAs.
  5. Price is above the 50-day MA.
  6. Price is at least ~25% above its 52-week low.
  7. Price is within ~25% of its 52-week high (closer is better).
  8. Relative Strength rank ≥ 70 (preferably 80s–90s) — i.e., it's outperforming most other stocks.

Why this matters: this is "top-right trading" — buying strength near the highs in a confirmed uptrend, not bottom-fishing. A perfect-looking VCP shape that's forming below the 200-day MA, down near the lows, is the single most common rookie mistake. The trend gate comes first.

🛡️ Stops & Position Sizing

Here's why Minervini loves the VCP: the tight final contraction lets him place a very close stop, which means he can take a meaningful position size while keeping dollar risk small.

Where the stop goes

  • Buy at the pivot; place the initial stop just below the low of the final contraction (the tight area you bought out of). Minervini
  • Because that final contraction is tight, your stop might be only a few percent away — a precise, logical risk level.

The tightness → size relationship (the key idea)

Dollar risk = (entry − stop) × number of shares. If you risk a fixed amount of your account per trade, then a tighter stop lets you buy more shares for the same risk. The VCP's tight pivot is what makes "back up the truck" sizing safe — the position is bigger, but the dollars at risk are still small.

Loss discipline Minervini / commonly cited

RuleDetail
Hard max lossCut around 7–8% in normal conditions; tighter (~5–6%) in tough markets; an absolute "uncle point" near 10%. commonly cited
Realized average lossBecause tight setups stop you out early, his actual average loss runs small (~3–4% in good markets). commonly cited
Risk per tradeA fixed slice of equity — roughly 0.5%–2.5% — small/test size first, scale up only as it works. commonly cited
BreakevenOnce up ~2R (twice your risk), never let a winner become a loser — move stop to breakeven, then trail. commonly cited
AddingAdd to winners only — never average down into a loser.
"It's better to lose correctly than to win incorrectly."
— Mark Minervini

Translation: a small loss taken by the rules is a good trade. A win you got by breaking your rules (moving a stop, oversizing) is a bad trade that will eventually cost you.

❌ When a VCP Is Disqualified or Fails

Disqualifiers — it's not a real VCP if…

  • Contractions don't tighten in sequence (a later pullback deeper than an earlier one).
  • Volume doesn't dry up — heavy volume on the down-legs means selling/distribution, not accumulation.
  • No Stage 2 trend — the base is below the 200-day MA / down near the lows (bottom-fishing).
  • Base is too loose/sloppy with no clean, obvious pivot.
  • Forms too fast (< ~3 weeks) or drags on with too many contractions.
  • Late-stage base (3rd/4th base of a long advance) — riskier; Minervini's rule: if you take one, "buy small positions so as not to get pummeled if they fail." Minervini

The breakout fails — now what?

  • Breakout on weak volume → suspect; often retail, not institutions.
  • Price reverses back below the pivot / last-contraction low → the line of least resistance has flipped down. Exit.
  • Honor the stop. Never widen it. Widening a violated stop is the fastest way to blow up.
  • Let it re-base. A failed setup can form a fresh base in a few weeks — a broken trade today doesn't mean the stock is dead. Re-evaluate later.
  • Multiple attempts are normal. The best stocks sometimes take 2–4 tries before the breakout sticks — each attempt risked small.
"If you find yourself getting stopped out repeatedly, either your stock-selection criteria are flawed, or the general market environment is hostile."
— Mark Minervini

Two things people wrongly credit to Minervini

• "Don't argue with the market" comes from Livermore / Reminiscences of a Stock Operator, not Minervini.
• The "wedging-up handle" faulty-base rule is a William O'Neil / IBD teaching, often taught alongside VCP but not specifically Minervini's. We'll teach those under their real authors.

🔬 Worked Examples — Three Charts to Dissect

Minervini teaches on stocks, so these canonical examples are equities — but the read is identical to crypto. For each, we'll do exactly what you'll do live: count the contractions, write the footprint, find the pivot, place the stop, and judge the volume. Apply the crypto checklist (next tab) as you go.

Example 1 — WFRD (Weatherford): reading the footprint

Two separate VCPs formed in the same stock — you read each base on its own. In plain English:

  • VCP #1 (the big base on the left): ran about 19 weeks with 4 pullbacks, each shallower than the last — roughly 19% → 15% → 13% → 7%. Shaped like an ascending triangle.
  • VCP #2 (the small base on the right): a quick ~3 weeks with just 2 pullbacks — about 7% then 4.5%. A tight symmetrical triangle.

That shorthand you saw earlier (19W 19/7 4T) decoded: 19W = 19 weeks long · 19/7 = biggest pullback 19%, smallest 7% · 4T = 4 contractions. It's just an optional way to jot down what a base looks like — you don't have to use it.

Dissect it: each pullback is shallower than the one before, down-day volume never tops up-day volume and dries up into the breakout, and the pivot is the high of the final contraction. Minervini

Crypto read: identical on a BTC or ETH daily — just confirm Bitcoin's own trend is up first, and check the coin's strength on its /BTC pair.

Example 2 — AEO (American Eagle): the pivot & the breakout

This is the textbook entry picture. AEO trends up, then coils into a tight contraction right under a clear ceiling — the flat blue line is the pivot.

  • Volume dries up inside the base — the chart marks the "lowest volume day during the VCP" right before the breakout. Sellers are exhausted.
  • The pivot is the top of that final contraction (the flat blue resistance line).
  • The entry is the break up through the pivot — marked "higher volume day during breakout." Price clears the line on a volume surge = real demand stepping in. That's the trigger.

Dissect it: quiet, low-volume tightening → break through the pivot on expanding volume. No volume surge on the break, no trade. Minervini

Crypto read: same trigger on BTC/ETH — but confirm with a candle close through the pivot (not just a wick), and check the volume surge is real spot buying, not just perp churn.

Example 3 — AM (Antero Midstream): shakeout, squat & managing the stop

This is the messy-reality example — two things that scare beginners out of good trades, and how to handle both.

  • Shakeout (circled): inside the base, price dips below the recent swing low — tripping the stops of people who bought there (their SL is marked just under it) — then snaps right back. That fast recovery is a sign of strength, not a reason to bail.
  • Breakout → Entry: price pushes up through the top of the base (the pivot) and you enter, initial stop under the last contraction.
  • Squat: the breakout briefly stalls and dips back toward the line — Minervini's word for a wobbly, false-looking breakout. Because your stop sits below, you simply hold and let it resolve.
  • New SL: once it's clearly working, the stop is trailed up under the most recent contraction to lock in the improving position.

Dissect it: shakeouts and squats are normal — the pre-set stop is exactly what lets you sit through them without panic-selling, and you trail it up only once price proves itself. Minervini

Crypto read: crypto wicks below obvious levels constantly to grab stops — this is that "stop-hunt." Set the stop a touch beyond the obvious swing low, prefer a candle close for confirmation, and don't get shaken out by a low-volume wick.

₿ Applying VCP to Crypto

This is the part that matters most for us. Minervini built the VCP on stocks, but the engine of the pattern is pure supply & demand — sellers drying up, weak hands handing off to strong hands — and that psychology is identical in crypto. So the shape and the logic translate directly to BTC, ETH and liquid large-caps. What changes is the environment: 24/7 trading, no earnings, Bitcoin-driven correlation, fragmented volume, and leverage. Let's go through each properly.

✅ What carries over unchanged

  • The contraction sequence — each pullback roughly half the last (e.g. 25% → 12% → 6%).
  • Volume drying up into a tight, quiet final contraction.
  • The pivot = high of the final contraction; enter on the breakout as volume expands.
  • Stop just below the last contraction; tight base → tight stop → controlled risk.
  • Trend-first — never buy a VCP in a downtrend or below your long-term MAs (your Class 20 MA framework applies directly).

Crypto reality #1 — Bitcoin is your "general market"

Minervini refuses to buy breakouts in a hostile market. In crypto, the "market" you check first isn't the S&P — it's Bitcoin. ~70–80% of an altcoin's move is just its correlation (beta) to BTC. commonly cited

  • Gate every alt VCP on the BTC chart. If BTC is in a confirmed uptrend (above your 200-day, higher highs/lows — your Class 12 structure work), alt VCPs are tradeable. If BTC is rolling over or sitting under a major resistance, stand down — the cleanest alt base will still get dragged down when BTC dumps.
  • Watch the regime signals you already know: BTC dominance (rising dominance = money leaving alts), and funding rates (from Class 19) — euphoric, very high positive funding across the board is a risk-off warning even if the chart looks great.
  • If Bitcoin itself is coiled in a tight base, wait. When BTC finally makes its big move, it drags every alt with it — so even a clean alt breakout can get overruled by Bitcoin's move. Let BTC break first, then trade the alts in the same direction.

Crypto reality #2 — Is the coin stronger than Bitcoin?

Minervini only buys leaders — names outperforming the overall market. In crypto, "the market" is Bitcoin, so a leader is simply a coin that's stronger than BTC itself.

How to check it in 10 seconds: on TradingView, switch the symbol from the dollar chart (e.g. SOLUSDT) to the Bitcoin-priced chart (SOLBTC). That shows the coin's price measured in Bitcoin instead of dollars.

  • If the SOLBTC chart is flat or rising → the coin is keeping up with or beating Bitcoin → leader, good.
  • If it's falling → the coin is weaker than Bitcoin, even when its dollar chart looks fine (because everything went up when BTC went up) → laggard, skip it.

Bottom line: trade coins that are strong against Bitcoin — not coins that only look strong because Bitcoin dragged them up.

Crypto reality #3 — Reading volume in a 24/7, fragmented market

Volume dry-up is the heart of the VCP, and crypto volume is the messiest part of the translation. Be deliberate:

  • Use one deep venue or an aggregated feed. A single small exchange's volume is noise. Stick to major-venue or aggregated volume so the "dry-up then surge" is real, not an artifact.
  • Ignore wash-traded low-caps. Fake volume makes a fake dry-up. If you can't trust the volume, you can't trade the VCP — skip it.
  • Use Volume Delta / CVD (Class 14), not just raw volume. In the final contraction you want to see selling pressure fading (delta flattening); on the breakout you want a genuine surge in buy-side delta, not just total volume from both sides.

Spot vs. perps — what crypto "volume" is actually telling you

This deserves its own class (and it's getting one) — but you need the core idea to read a crypto VCP's volume correctly. The same coin trades in two different markets at once:

  • Spot — buying the actual coin. Real ownership changes hands. This is "real" demand.
  • Perps (perpetual futures) — leveraged bets on the price, settled in stablecoins; no coin actually changes hands (this is the leverage market from Class 19).

The reality: perp volume is usually much BIGGER than spot

On most coins, perp (derivatives) volume dwarfs spot volume — often several times larger. So the volume bars on a typical crypto chart are mostly leverage activity, not real accumulation. That's the opposite of stocks, where the volume you see is real shares changing hands. It means you can't take a crypto "volume surge" at face value the way Minervini does on equities — you have to ask where the volume is coming from.

Why it matters for a VCP breakout:

  • A breakout where the surge shows up in spot buying (real buyers taking ownership) is higher quality and tends to hold.
  • A breakout that's purely perp-driven can be fast but fragile — leverage unwinds just as fast (a long squeeze), and the move fails / becomes a squat.

The tools that reveal it (don't worry about mastering these today — this is exactly what the dedicated class covers):

  • Open Interest (OI) — the total size of open perp positions. Price up + OI up = new leveraged money committing (fuels the move, but also fuels a future squeeze). Price up + OI down = mostly shorts covering, often less durable.
  • Funding rate (Class 19) — what longs pay shorts. Very high positive funding into a breakout = crowded longs = squeeze risk. Calm/neutral funding on a breakout is healthier.
  • Spot CVD vs perp CVD (Class 14) — shows whether the buying pressure is coming from spot or from perps. Spot-led = real demand.
  • Perp premium / basis — perps trading well above spot = leveraged FOMO; trading near spot = calmer, more sustainable.

VCP breakout rule of thumb for crypto

  • Trust it more when: spot buying leads, Open Interest rises moderately, and funding is not extreme.
  • Be cautious when: spot volume is flat/declining and the breakout is carried by perps with spiking funding — high odds of a squat or failed breakout.

For today, the one takeaway: crypto "volume" is mostly leverage — the breakout you trust is the one backed by real spot buying. We'll go deep on OI, funding, CVD and liquidations in the dedicated class.

Crypto reality #4 — Timeframes, candles & the "close"

  • Pick a close and be consistent. Crypto never closes, so define your candle — the daily UTC close (00:00 UTC) is the standard reference; the weekly close matters most for majors. Don't pivot between conventions to justify a trade.
  • Timeframe by horizon: swing-trade VCPs on the daily; majors (BTC/ETH) base beautifully on the weekly; the 4H works for faster alt setups but expect more noise/false breakouts.
  • Crypto bases can form faster than equities — a clean VCP might take 3–6 weeks rather than 6–12. The footprint still records whatever actually happened; just don't force a "VCP" onto a 3-day wiggle.
  • Weekends are thin. Low weekend liquidity produces both fake breakouts and fake breakdowns. Many crypto traders weight the weekday/US-session breakout more heavily and treat weekend moves with suspicion.

Crypto reality #5 — Liquidity, venue & the pivot's "fakeout" problem

⚠️ Stop-hunts / liquidity grabs below the pivot

Crypto is notorious for wicking just below an obvious level to trigger stops, then reversing — exactly at the pivot/last-contraction low where VCP traders place stops. Defenses: (1) prefer a candle-close beyond the level over an intraday wick, (2) place the stop a touch beyond the obvious round number / swing low rather than right on it, and (3) size so a stop-hunt wick doesn't blow your risk budget.

  • Liquid names only. BTC, ETH, and top-tier large-caps. Low-cap "textbook VCPs" are often illiquid traps: fake volume, air-pocket gaps, and slippage that wrecks your planned risk.
  • Mind your venue / slippage. On thin books your entry above the pivot can fill far worse than planned. CEX spot, deep perps, or a liquid DEX like Hyperliquid (Class 3) — not a low-liquidity pair.

Crypto reality #6 — Stops, leverage & funding

  • The VCP's gift is a tight, logical invalidation (just under the last contraction). That's exactly the level to anchor a stop — and if you use leverage, to anchor it away from your liquidation price (Class 19). Let the pattern define risk; don't over-leverage so that normal noise liquidates you.
  • Prefer isolated margin for a single setup so a failure can't cascade into the rest of your book (Class 19).
  • Funding is a holding cost. Minervini says "time works against a short"; in crypto, longs also bleed funding when it's positive. A VCP that breaks out and goes nowhere costs you funding while you wait — another reason the breakout should move promptly or be cut.
  • Volatility-scale your size. Use an ATR-based stop (Class 18) so a wider crypto range automatically gives you a smaller position at the same dollar risk.

✅ The crypto VCP pre-trade checklist

  • Bitcoin is in an uptrend (or at least not breaking down) and not sitting under major resistance.
  • The coin shows relative strength vs BTC (holding up / higher lows on the /BTC pair).
  • It's a liquid name with trustworthy volume.
  • 2–4 contractions, each tighter than the last, on a clean base.
  • Volume dries up into the final, quiet contraction (confirm with Delta/CVD).
  • One clean, obvious pivot (high of the last contraction).
  • Stop level is defined (just below the last contraction) and your size/leverage keeps risk small and away from liquidation.
  • Entry on a volume-backed breakout (prefer a candle close through the pivot over an intraday wick).

➡️ See the "Worked Examples" tab for three real, dissected charts (WFRD, NU, AM). Read each against the crypto checklist above — the pattern reads identically; only the market gate (Bitcoin), the /BTC relative-strength check, and the volume caveats change.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  1. A VCP is volatility + volume contracting before a breakout. Swings shrink left-to-right, volume dries up — a coiled spring. It's the effect of supply/demand resolving.
  2. Read the footprint: weeks, biggest/smallest contraction %, and the count (e.g., 19W 19/7 4T). Typically 2–6 contractions (3–4 is clean), each ~half the prior.
  3. The pivot is the high of the final, tightest contraction — the line of least resistance. Enter on the breakout with a volume surge; buy on the way up, don't chase.
  4. Context first (SEPA + Trend Template). Only trade VCPs in confirmed Stage 2 uptrends, in strong leaders. The trend gate beats the prettiest shape.
  5. Tight base = tight stop = controlled, sizable risk. Stop just below the last contraction; cut fast, never widen, add only to winners.
  6. In crypto: the pattern translates, but trend-gate against BTC, use clean volume, stick to liquid names, and respect higher volatility.
